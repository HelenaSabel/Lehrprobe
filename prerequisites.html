Concepts to know before following the lesson

Textual criticism: terminology and principles

In the words of Shillingsburg (1996: 4):

Textual criticism is often understood as the art of examining literary texts to authenticate the words, their order, spelling, punctuation, and capitalization. Authenticated texts are generally thought to be reliable for reading and studying literary works. Thus, textual critics are valued because they serve the laudable function of providing authenticated texts.

To complete Shillingsburg description, we may add that the need to provide an “authenticated text” is also part of Diplomatics, thus the methods used within textual criticism are also applicable to non-literary documents.

The critical method: principles

Through specific techniques, textual criticism investigates the genesis and evolution of a work, identifying its various forms and its transformations over time (Chiesa 2002: 11). Traditionally, these techniques are divided into two distinct phases: the recensio, in which the witnesses of a text are evaluated, and the constitutio textus, which consists on providing the authenticated text, “a hypothesis of a text conforming to what the orginal should have been on the basis of the results of the recensio” (Chiesa 2002: 51-52, emphasis and translation mine).

Recensio

The first step is the compilation of sources relating to a text (tradition). The tradition may be of two types:

  • direct tradition: manuscripts (or printed)
  • indirect tradition: comments, quotations, translations, imitations...

Depending on the number of preserved witnesses, the paths of the editor are diversified. A distinction must be made between:

  • traditions with a single witness (codex unicus). The work is transcribed, which undergoes a process of examinatio, i.e. word division, resolution of abbreviations, punctuation, correction of mechanical errors, emendatio.
  • tradition with several witnesses. The relationships between the witnesses must be established. A e will be carried out to establish the variae lectiones or variants.

The editor must the variants collected during the collatio, and select the loci critici that will allow her/him to classify the witnesses.

The only clue is to let oneself be guided by the obvious errors, considering a series of principles:

  • any deviation from the original constitutes an error –involuntary mistakes made by the copyist during the copying process– or an innovation –the copyist consciously varies the lesson of his model–;
  • errors and innovations constitute an irrefutable proof to relate the different witnesses of a work. Its presence marks the sources derived from the manuscript in which the error or innovation occurred, allowing the grouping into families;
  • there are two main types of variants: equipolent readings, all readings are correct in principle, and errors (opoposed to correct readings). If one or more witnesses read independently from others, they have lectiones singulares (equipolent readings or errors);
  • equipolent readings do not allow us to affiliate the witnesses; they must be used to corroborate filiations. The affiliation is done based on the errors;
  • a singular lesson may be the authentic one against the lesson of the other witnesses.

As said above, the only valid criterion for affiliation is the significant common error. There are two types:

  • common conjunctive errors. They are found in two or more witnesses; they could not have been committed by the copyists independently. They are a guarantee of connection between the copies that have them;
  • common separative errors. They are those which a copyist cannot notice or correct by conjecture or other witnesses. Its presence in one witnesses and its absence in others indicates independence of the former with respect to the latter.

The correct classification of errors is fundamental in an edition:

  • omissions due to eye-skipping, transpositions and equipolent readings are not significant;
  • gaps not derived from a homeoteleuton and repetitions of a word prefixed or postponed by addition, apart from in its place, in another place, are decisive.

Significant errors can eventually prove:

  • the independence of a witness with respect to others;
  • the connection of two or more witnesses against another;
  • the descent of all the witnesses from a common ancestor other than the original.

After the examination of the variants, the stemma codicum is drawn, which formalises the relationships between the copies that preserve a text.

The stemma includes the codices descripti (and contamination). These are only removed in the constitutio textus stage.

What is represented on a stemma?: families or groups of witnesses (see Fig. 1 with an example).

  • Family: set of witnesses characterized by the presence of common conjunctive errors, which depend on a common branch, an ascendant (archetype or subarchetype). The association of the codices is corroborated by the presence of readings that are characteristic of each family;
  • Ascendant: name given to the manuscript on which depends a group of witnesses that make up a family, or the totality of the witnesses of a work.

Consider:

  • If the ascendant is preserved, the derived copies have no stemmatic value: these are the codices descripti; they are removed from the stemma (eliminatio codicum descriptorum).
  • If the ascendant has been lost, it is called codex interpositus. It is reconstructed through the comparative analysis of the characteristic variants of the family of preserved manuscripts. When this codex represents the antecedent of only one family of witnesses it is called a subarchetype. When it is the antecedent of all preserved manuscripts it is called an archetype (i.e. original). The existence of an archetype is demonstrable when all witnesses offer at least one common conjunctive error.

Constitutio textus

After the recensio phase, the editor can face two possible situations:

  • s/he was able to affiliate all the witnesses and draw a stemma codicum;
  • s/he could not determine the relationships –or only some– between the witnesses, and thus the stemma could not be drawn.

In both cases, the variants will be examined and selected, operating in each case in a particular way.

At the stage of the constitutio textus, the archetype cannot be a preserved codex. If preserved, the other witnesses will be codices descripti, the stemma thus disappears when reduced to a codex unicus. The archetype must be understood as a lost, specific codex that we can reconstruct from the preserved witnesses.

The reconstruction of the archetype cannot be done in a completely mechanical way: the reading determined by the stemma would be erroneously accepted in all cases.

At this stage the iudicium must intervene: it will be necessary to choose between the equipolent variants of the recensio. The editor makes use of internal data that allows her or him to privilege one reading over another: this is a examinatio cum iudicio.

The editor must analyse each of the loci critici. The stemma must serve to corroborate the selection of the variant, which must be done, above all, based on a complete knowledge of the usus scribendi of the author and her or his time.

However, at times the editor might need to resolve a locus criticus with a particular solution that it is not based on the readings of the manuscripts: this is called emendatio ope ingenii in which the interpretation of the editor plays a key role. The emendatio must be done attending to different criteria:

  • lectio difficilior probior (prefer the more difficult reading)
  • lectio brevior potior (favor the briefer reading)
  • usus scribendi (editors should be sensitive to norms and habits that are typical of times or places or schools of writing)
  • conformatio textus (the context: the editor evaluates the lost or addition of particules (e.g. negations, clitics, etc.), a frequent phenomenon during the process of copying a witness)
  • res metrica (when the work is in verse, the editor evaluates cases of hypometry and hypermetry)
