Concepts to know before following the lesson

Textual criticism: terminology and principles

In the words of Shillingsburg (1996: 4):

Textual criticism is often understood as the art of examining literary texts to authenticate the words, their order, spelling, punctuation, and capitalization. Authenticated texts are generally thought to be reliable for reading and studying literary works. Thus, textual critics are valued because they serve the laudable function of providing authenticated texts.

To complete Shillingsburg description, it is necessary to add that the need to provide an “authenticated text” is also part of Diplomatics so the methods used within textual criticism are applicable to non-literary documents.

The critical method: principles

Through specific techniques, textual criticism investigates the genesis and evolution of a work, identifying its various forms and its transformations over time (Chiesa 2002: 11). Traditionally, these techniques are divided into two distinct phases: the recensio, in which the witnesses of a text are evaluated, and the constitutio textus, which consists on providing the authenticated text, “a hypothesis of a text conforming to what the orginal should have been on the basis of the results of the recensio” (Chiesa 2002: 51-52, my translation).

The first step is the compilation of sources relating to a text (tradition). The tradition may be of two types:

  • direct tradition: manuscripts (or printed)
  • indirect tradition: comments, quotations, translations, imitations...

Depending on the number of saved witnesses preserved, the paths of the editor are diversified. A distinction must be made between:

  • traditions with a single witness (codex unicus). The work is transcribed, which undergoes a process of examinatio, i.e. word division, resolution of abbreviations, punctuation, correction of mechanical errors, emendatio.
  • tradition with several witnesses. The relationships between the witnesses must be established. A collatio will be carried out to establish the variae lectiones or variants.

The editor must the variants collected during the collatio, and select the loci critici that will allow her/him to classify the witnesses.

The only clue is to let oneself be guided by the obvious errors, considering a series of principles:

  • any deviation from the original constitutes an error –involuntary mistakes made by the copyist during the copying process– or an innovation –the copyist consciously varies the lesson of his model–;
  • errors and innovations constitute an irrefutable proof to relate the different witnesses of a work. Its presence marks the sources derived from the manuscript in which the error or innovation occurred, allowing the grouping into families;
  • there are two main types of variants: equipolent readings, all readings are correct in principle, and errors (opoposed to correct readings). If one or more witnesses read independently from others, they have lectiones singulares (equipolent readings or errors);
  • equipolent readings do not allow us to affiliate the witnesses; they must be used to corroborate filiations. The affiliation is done based on the errors;
  • a singular lesson may be the authentic one against the lesson of the other witnesses.

As said above, the only valid criterion for affiliation is the significant common error. There are two types:

  • common conjunctive errors. They are found in two or more witnesses; they could not have been committed by the copyists independently. They are a guarantee of connection between the copies that have them;
  • common separative errors. They are those which a copyist cannot notice or correct by conjecture or other witnesses. Its presence in one witnesses and its absence in others indicates independence of the former with respect to the latter.

The correct classification of errors is fundamental in an edition:

  • omissions due to eye-skipping, transpositions and equipolent readings are not significant;
  • gaps not derived from a homeoteleuton and repetitions of a word prefixed or postponed by addition, apart from in its place, in another place, are decisive.

Significant errors can eventually prove:

  • the independence of a witness with respect to others;
  • the connection of two or more witnesses against another;
  • the descent of all the witnesses from a common ancestor other than the original.
